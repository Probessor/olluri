The beginning

How I fell in love with startups

I grew up in Bergen, where fjords and fishing boats outnumber tech hubs — but ambition was never in short supply. My first brush with entrepreneurship was watching my father navigate the ups and downs of running a small maritime business. That early exposure to the drama of building something from scratch never left me.

At BI Norwegian Business School in Oslo, I stumbled into an entrepreneurship program almost by accident. What started as an elective turned into an obsession. I began attending startup meetups, writing about new companies for a student newsletter, and generally becoming the person who showed up to every event involving founders.

After graduating, I joined a small venture studio in Oslo as a generalist — which meant doing everything from financial modelling to event logistics. It was chaotic, underpaid, and the best education I've ever received.

Today

What I'm focused on right now

The Norwegian startup ecosystem is at an inflection point. Green energy, AI, maritime tech — there's more high-quality deal flow coming out of Oslo and Trondheim than ever before. But international investors still underestimate it, and brilliant Norwegian founders still struggle to access the capital they deserve.

That gap is exactly where I operate. In 2026, I'm particularly excited about the convergence of Norway's natural resources, world-class research institutions, and a new generation of technically fearless founders building genuinely hard things.

I write about all of this on my Insights page, speak at events across Europe, and take on a limited number of scouting and consulting engagements each quarter to stay genuinely close to the companies I work with.

What I believe

The principles that guide my work

Relationships over transactions. Every introduction I make is one I can stand behind. I'd rather make fewer, better connections than build a conveyor belt of introductions that go nowhere.

Honest opinions, even uncomfortable ones. Founders don't need more cheerleaders — they need honest feedback. If I think a pitch needs work before it hits investors, I'll say so.

Local roots, global mindset. Norwegian founders build differently. Understanding that cultural context isn't just useful — it's essential. I help translate that to an international audience who hasn't yet learned to read the signals.
